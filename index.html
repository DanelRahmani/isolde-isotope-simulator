What is an Atom?

Everything you see, touch, and breathe is made of atoms — the basic building blocks of matter. Each atom has a tiny, dense nucleus at its centre, made of protons (positive charge) and neutrons (no charge). Whizzing around the nucleus are electrons (negative charge), arranged in shells. The number of protons defines which element it is (1 = hydrogen, 6 = carbon, 79 = gold). The number of neutrons can vary — these variants are called isotopes.

6 protons + 6 neutrons + 6 electrons

While about 250 isotopes are stable in nature, physicists have predicted thousands more radioactive ones that decay in fractions of a second. Studying these exotic nuclei — the domain of nuclear physics — helps us understand why matter exists the way it does.

How does ISOLDE work?

ISOLDE (Isotope Separator On-Line DEvice) has operated at CERN since 1967. It fires a high-energy proton beam from CERN's accelerator complex into a heavy metal target. The collision shatters atomic nuclei, creating a spray of exotic radioactive isotopes that don't exist naturally on Earth. Then, powerful magnetic fields separate these isotopes by mass — like a prism splitting light into colours, but for atoms. ISOLDE has produced and studied over 1,300 different isotopes.

By isolating individual isotopes, scientists can measure their mass, shape, decay pattern, and internal structure — revealing how the strong nuclear force binds protons and neutrons together in extreme conditions.

Why does this matter?

  • Nuclear astrophysics: Understanding how elements are forged inside stars and supernovae.
  • Medical isotopes: Many isotopes studied at ISOLDE have become tools for cancer diagnosis and treatment.
  • Fundamental physics: Testing the limits of our theories about how matter behaves at the smallest scales.
  • Materials science: Implanting isotopes into materials to study their properties at the atomic level.
