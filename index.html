What is an Atom?

Everything you see, touch, and breathe is made of atoms — the basic building blocks of matter. Each atom has a tiny, dense nucleus at its centre, made of protons (positive charge) and neutrons (no charge). Whizzing around the nucleus are electrons (negative charge), arranged in shells. The number of protons defines which element it is (1 = hydrogen, 6 = carbon, 79 = gold). The number of neutrons can vary — these variants are called isotopes.

While about 250 isotopes are stable in nature, physicists have predicted thousands more radioactive ones that decay in fractions of a second. Studying these exotic nuclei — the domain of nuclear physics — helps us understand why matter exists.

How does ISOLDE work?

ISOLDE (Isotope Separator On-Line DEvice) has operated at CERN since 1967. It fires a high-energy proton beam into a heavy metal target. The collision shatters atomic nuclei, creating exotic radioactive isotopes that don''t exist naturally on Earth. Powerful magnetic fields then separate these isotopes by mass — like a prism splitting light into colours, but for atoms. ISOLDE has produced over 1,300 different isotopes.

By isolating individual isotopes, scientists measure their mass, shape, decay, and internal structure — revealing how the strong nuclear force binds matter in extreme conditions.

Why does this matter?

  • Nuclear astrophysics: Understanding how elements are forged inside stars and supernovae.
  • Medical isotopes: Many ISOLDE isotopes became tools for cancer diagnosis and treatment.
  • Fundamental physics: Testing the limits of our theories of matter at the smallest scales.
  • Materials science: Implanting isotopes into materials to study atomic-level properties.
